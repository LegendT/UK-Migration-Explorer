Migration statistics come wrapped in contested vocabulary. A site that corrects other
people's language owes an account of its own.

## Rules that are precision

These are not matters of taste. Getting them wrong makes a sentence factually wrong.

| Instead of | We write | Because |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Immigration is X" | Net migration / visa grants / asylum applications is X | Naming the measured quantity is the difference between a fact and a category error |
| "The backlog" | The initial decision backlog / the appeals backlog / total casework | There are several queues, of different sizes, moving in different directions |
| "Asylum seekers entered illegally" | People who claimed asylum after arriving by an irregular route | Route of arrival and claim status are separate facts, and one does not determine the other |
| A figure with no period | The figure with its period and basis attached | Without those, a number on this subject carries no information |

## Rules that are choices

These are defensible, and they are still choices. We would rather say so than imply that
everyone reasonable agrees.

**We write "irregular arrival" rather than "illegal migrant" by default.** The phrase we
avoid is legally imprecise, arriving without prior permission is not in itself what makes a
person's presence unlawful, and a claim's outcome is decided separately. But it is also true
that our preferred phrasing is the one favoured by people who are broadly sympathetic to
migrants, and that some readers will hear the softer term as taking a side. That is a fair
observation and we are not going to pretend otherwise.

**We avoid "flooding", "swamping" and similar.** This is a judgement that metaphors drawn
from natural disasters describe people badly. It is not a statistical rule.

**We describe people as people.** The site publishes what the asylum system spends. It does
not publish a cost per asylum seeker, because dividing a system's running costs by the
number of people in it produces a figure that reads as a person's value.

## Where our sources use different words

We quote source titles exactly as published, including the words we would not choose.

The Home Office dataset behind our irregular-arrival figures was titled "Irregular migration
to the UK detailed dataset (illegal entry routes)" and was renamed "Illegal entry routes to
the UK detailed dataset" from the year ending December 2025 edition. We cite whichever title
the edition we used actually carries.

Renaming a source quietly would be concealment, and a reader who followed the link would be
right to distrust everything else on the page.

## The labels on our claim checks

Each claim check is tagged with a direction: **restrictionist** for claims that overstate the
scale or cost of migration, and **pro-migration** for claims that overstate its benefits or
understate its difficulties. Those are the only two labels in use. A misuse common on both
sides is currently written up under whichever version circulates more, and named as shared
in the text.

These labels describe *the claim*, not the person making it, and they are ours rather than
anybody's self-description. "Restrictionist" in particular is not a word most people who
make those claims would use about themselves. We use it because "anti-immigration" is worse
and because we need a consistent handle, not because it is neutral. If you have a better
pair, we would genuinely like to hear it.

## What we do not do

We do not attribute claims to named people. Our claim checks describe propositions that
circulate, not quotations. That avoids litigating individuals and it carries a real cost:
the "nobody actually says that" rebuttal. We accept the trade and would revisit it if that
rebuttal became the main criticism of the site.

We do not correct claims about what policy *should* be. That is a question about values, and
this site has no standing to answer it.
